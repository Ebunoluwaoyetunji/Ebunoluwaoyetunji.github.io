01 · Fintech · Mobile App

Making a financial decision
feel less like a form.

Nexapay is a live fintech platform designed to make financial access feel straightforward. I was responsible for designing the full product experience — including the most complex journey on the platform: the account tier upgrade flow.

Role
UI/UX Designer
Duration
Dec 2024 – Feb 2026
Deliverables
50+ screens · Design system · Prototype
Status
Live · 50+ users at launch

The Problem

Users didn't understand the value of upgrading — or why their data was needed.

When I analysed the account upgrade journey, the core friction wasn't the steps themselves — it was that users arrived at the flow without understanding the value exchange. They were being asked to submit personal documents (NIN slip, selfie) with no clear sense of what they'd get in return, or why it was necessary.

The compliance requirement felt like a wall, not a gate to something better. My job was to reframe it — and to do that, I had to understand both what the users needed and what the regulations actually required.

The Tier Upgrade Flow

The Approach

Lead the user toward the decision — not just through the steps.

I redesigned the entry point to show users exactly where they were (Tier 1), what they were missing (higher limits, more balance), and what it would take to get there — before asking for anything. The tier comparison screen became a clarity tool, not a compliance notice.

Each subsequent step (NIN entry, selfie capture) was framed as progress toward something, with clear language about why each piece of information was needed and how it would be protected. The goal was to make a regulatory requirement feel like a product feature, not a bureaucratic hurdle.

The Cards Screen

Balance, transactions, and quick actions — all in one place.

The Cards screen was designed to give users a complete picture of their financial activity without overwhelming them. Balance is prominently displayed, the card visual acts as a trust anchor, and recent transactions are immediately visible — all actionable with Top-Up and Withdraw a single tap away. Every transaction state (confirmed, pending, failed, reversed) is visually distinct without relying on colour alone.

What I Learned

Compliance and clarity are not opposites.

The most important lesson from this project was that regulatory requirements don't have to feel regulatory. If you frame the requirement as a benefit — "verify your NIN to access higher limits" rather than "NIN verification required" — the user's mental model shifts from obstacle to opportunity.

Working within a cross-functional team that included compliance meant I had to understand the constraints fully before I could design around them. That's a skill I carry into every project now.
